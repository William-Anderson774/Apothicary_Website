· FORMULAE ET REMEDIA ·


These compounds are drawn from historical apothecary records, herbal manuscripts, and folk medicine traditions spanning the 13th through 19th centuries. They are offered here as archival documents — a window into the practice — and not as medical instruction.

No. 01
Sleep Draught
Potio soporis
Sleep Anxiety Nervous System
Ingredients
  • Valerian root, dried2 tsp
  • Chamomile flowers1 tbsp
  • Lemon balm leaf1 tsp
  • Lavender flowers½ tsp
  • Raw honey1 tsp
  • Water, freshly boiled8 oz
Preparation

Combine the dried valerian root, chamomile, lemon balm, and lavender in a muslin pouch or loose tea strainer. Place in a warmed ceramic vessel. Pour freshly boiled water over the herbs and cover immediately to capture the volatile oils.

Allow to steep for no fewer than ten minutes — valerian requires longer than most herbs to release its full sedative compounds. Remove the herbs and stir in honey while the draught is still warm. Drink one hour before retiring, in a quiet room, away from candlelight.

Recorded in the London Pharmacopoeia of 1618. Valerian was so widely prescribed for sleeplessness during the 17th century that apothecaries kept it in bulk at the front of the shop, alongside pepper and salt.

No. 02
Fever Breaking Tea
Infusio febrifuga
Fever Immunity Diaphoretic
Ingredients
  • Yarrow, dried aerial parts2 tsp
  • Elderflower, dried2 tsp
  • Peppermint leaf1 tsp
  • Ginger root, grated fresh¼ tsp
  • Water, freshly boiled10 oz
Preparation

This is a diaphoretic compound — its purpose is to encourage perspiration, which the old apothecaries understood as the body's mechanism for expelling heat. Combine all herbs in a covered vessel and steep for fifteen minutes. The covering is essential; do not let the steam escape.

Strain and serve hot. The patient should be abed, warmly covered, and drink the full measure slowly. A second cup may be prepared after two hours if the fever persists. Rest is considered as important as the draught itself.

This combination — yarrow, elderflower, peppermint — was so standard across European herbalism that it was sometimes called the "Holy Trinity" of fever management. Nicholas Culpeper described yarrow as "an especial herb for fevers that come of blood" in 1652.

No. 03
Wound Salve
Unguentum vulnerarium
Wounds Skin Antiseptic
Ingredients
  • Calendula flowers, dried¼ cup
  • St. John's Wort flowers2 tbsp
  • Lavender essential oil15 drops
  • Beeswax, grated1 oz
  • Olive oil, cold pressed4 oz
Preparation

First, prepare an infused oil: place the dried calendula and St. John's Wort in a clean glass jar and cover completely with olive oil. Seal and set in a warm, sunny windowsill for four to six weeks, shaking daily. Strain through muslin, pressing all oil from the spent flowers.

Warm the infused oil gently over a double boiler. Add the grated beeswax and stir until fully melted. Remove from heat, allow to cool slightly, then stir in the lavender oil. Pour immediately into small tins or jars and allow to set without disturbance. Apply sparingly to clean wounds, burns, or irritated skin.

Calendula salves have been documented in European apothecary records since at least the 12th century, appearing in the writings of Hildegard von Bingen. The combination with St. John's Wort oil — known as Hypericum oil — for nerve-adjacent wounds became popular in the 16th century.

No. 04
Digestive Bitters
Tinctura amara
Digestion Liver Tincture
Ingredients
  • Gentian root, dried1 tsp
  • Dandelion root, dried2 tsp
  • Ginger root, dried1 tsp
  • Orange peel, dried1 tbsp
  • Cardamom pods, crushed4 pods
  • Brandy or grain alcohol8 oz
Preparation

Combine all dried botanicals in a clean glass jar with a tight-fitting lid. Pour the brandy over the herbs, ensuring all plant material is submerged by at least one inch of liquid. Seal and store in a cool, dark location for four weeks, turning or shaking the jar each day.

After four weeks, strain through a double layer of muslin into a clean bottle, pressing firmly to extract all tincture. Decant into small dropper bottles for use. Take 15 to 20 drops in a small amount of water approximately fifteen minutes before a heavy meal, or at the first sign of digestive discomfort.

Digestive bitters were among the most commercially significant products of the 19th-century apothecary. Gentian, the primary bittering agent, was considered essential enough to appear in virtually every European pharmacopoeia from the 1600s onward. Many modern aperitifs — Campari, Angostura — descend directly from these formulas.

No. 05
Elderberry Syrup
Syrupus sambuci
Immunity Antiviral Winter
Ingredients
  • Dried elderberries1 cup
  • Cinnamon stick1 whole
  • Cloves, whole3 pieces
  • Ginger root, fresh sliced1 inch
  • Raw honey1 cup
  • Water3 cups
Preparation

Combine elderberries, cinnamon, cloves, ginger, and water in a saucepan. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer uncovered until the liquid is reduced by roughly half — approximately 45 minutes. The berries will be quite soft. Remove from heat and allow to cool until just warm to the touch.

Strain through a fine mesh, pressing the berries firmly with the back of a spoon. Discard the spent solids. Once the liquid has cooled to below 40°C — honey's beneficial enzymes are destroyed by heat above this — stir in the raw honey until fully dissolved. Bottle in dark glass. Take one tablespoon daily through winter months, or two tablespoons at the onset of illness.

Elderberry preparations appear in English herbals as far back as John Gerard's Herball of 1597. The elder tree was considered so medicinally important in rural England that it was considered unlucky to cut one down, and apothecaries often grew elder bushes directly behind their shops.
